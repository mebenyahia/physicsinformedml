The Mass–Spring Equation

The system we study is a small block attached to a spring. When you pull the block and let go, it oscillates back and forth around its rest position. The displacement of the block from the rest position is called x(t), and it changes over time.

The motion is described by the differential equation:

m * x''(t) + c * x'(t) + k * x(t) = 0
  • m – the mass of the block. In our example we use m = 1.0 kilogram.
  • k – the spring stiffness. Larger k means a stiffer spring. We use k = 1.0 newton per meter.
  • c – the damping coefficient. This models friction or air resistance. We use c = 0.1, which leads to gently decaying oscillations.
  • x'(t) – the velocity, meaning how fast the displacement is changing.
  • x''(t) – the acceleration, the rate of change of the velocity.

The spring force always pulls the mass back toward the rest position. If the mass is moving, damping slows it down, acting like a gentle brake. With no damping (c = 0) you would see perfect sine-like oscillations. With damping, the oscillations shrink over time as energy is lost.

You can picture the scene as:

[wall]====spring====[mass]

Pull the mass to the right, release it, and it will move left, overshoot a bit, and repeat. The equation above is the mathematical summary of that story.

Understanding the variables makes it easier to follow the neural network training later on.
